From 246 to 6 — and Why It Stops There

The Maynard–Tao / Polymath8 bounded-gaps program pushes the twin-prime gap bound down a short ladder. Strip away the rigorous machinery and only two conjectural assumptions are actually doing the work — and they are the same conjecture at two strengths. Here is what each rung assumes, and how validatable each assumption is.

The ladder — what each rung assumes

H1 = lim inf (pn+1 − pn). Everything else — the multidimensional sieve, admissible-tuple search, the Mk optimization, the ε-enlargement — is rigorous and unconditional.
Bound on H1Rests onStatus of that input
≤ 246 Bombieri–Vinogradov theorem only (level θ = ½) ✓ Proven (1965)
≤ 12 Elliott–Halberstam conjecture (EH): primes at level θ → 1 ◯ Open
≤ 6 Generalized EH (GEH): same for convolutions α∗β ◯ Open — strictly stronger than EH
2 No sieve method reaches it (would be actual twin primes) △ Blocked by parity

How the rungs move: 246 uses only Bombieri–Vinogradov. EH lowers the Mk > 2/θ threshold to 2, met at k = 5 (tuple {0, 2, 6, 8, 12}, diameter 12). The baseline M3 ≈ 1.35 and even M4 = (4/3)log 4 ≈ 1.85 fall short of 2, so GEH is needed to license the ε-enlarged sieve that pushes M3 past 2 — giving tuple {0, 2, 6}, diameter 6.

Easier vs harder to validate

Ranked from already-done to needs-new-mathematics:

  1. 1

    Sieve machinery + Bombieri–Vinogradov

    ✓ Proven

    Admissible-tuple search and the Mk optimization are finite, computer-verified. Bombieri–Vinogradov gives level θ = ½ (theorem since 1965). This tier alone yields 246.

  2. 2

    Beyond ½ for smooth moduli — evidence for EH

    ◐ Partial

    Zhang's θ = ½ + 1/584, refined by Polymath8a to ½ + 7/300 — proven, but only for smooth squarefree moduli, via Deligne's Weil-conjecture bounds plus Kloosterman / Birch–Bombieri sums. Deep, but real.

  3. 3

    Beyond ½ for convolutions — evidence for GEH

    ◐ Partial

    Bombieri–Friedlander–Iwaniec and Fouvry–Iwaniec proved equidistribution of convolutions α∗β past ½ (well-factorable level x4/7−ε, triple x3/5). Accessible precisely because convolutions are bilinear (Type II) — the structure current methods can exploit.

  4. 4

    Full EH and GEH

    ◯ Open

    Any fixed θ > ½ for the primes over all moduli (EH), and the strictly stronger GEH. Hard but well-posed targets — no technique reaches a single θ > ½ uniformly over general moduli.

  5. 5

    Below 6, toward 2

    △ No-go for sieves

    Not an equidistribution assumption at all. Selberg's parity obstruction caps every sieve method at 6 — even full GEH gives exactly 6. Reaching gap 2 needs genuinely new, parity-breaking mathematics.

The GEH paradox

Harder as a full conjecture

GEH ⇒ EH (the von Mangoldt function decomposes into convolutions), so GEH is strictly stronger. The rung that buys 12 → 6 rests on the heavier hypothesis.

Easier in the instances that matter

The only beyond-½ results we actually have live on the convolution (GEH-flavored) side, because Type II / bilinear structure is what the dispersion method digests. Primes-via-EH lack it at the needed level — and collide with parity.

Both are true: they describe different objects — a uniform, all-moduli, all-convolutions conjecture versus particular well-factorable instances.

Why it stops at 6

The parity wall

Sieves cannot tell an even from an odd number of prime factors, so they cannot isolate primes (one factor) from products of two primes. Polymath8b adapts Selberg's parity argument to show that 6 is the best any purely sieve-theoretic method can do — even on GEH.

Two caveats: this optimality is heuristic (it rests on the conjectural Möbius randomness law), and it forbids the sieve approach — not the existence of twin primes themselves.
